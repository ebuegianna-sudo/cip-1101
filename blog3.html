Why Technology Is More Human Than We Think

Written By: Penny Wrinkle

Date: 02/03/2026





CS, A Bird Cage for Math Addicts?

Majority of society characterize programmers, developers, and researchers as total nerds who constantly wear jackets and crunch numbers for a living. There is some truth to that, some of them just hide it better. As a computer science student, this course feels like a math major in disguise and a living prison as someone who loves too much freedom. I have no clear goal for myself yet. Technology just seems so robotic. Nurses, doctors, or maybe diplomats save and inspire lives which is a fulfilling reason to keep going besides earning money. But what could be fulfilling about being a person in technology? What is human about technology? I’ve recently realized what could be and what I could be. And I will tell you with this story.



Purpose Born From a Noble Testimony

Recently I attended a funeral. A church-mate of mine died from cancer and she was a breadwinner for her family all at the same time. Her testimony, the way she lived, truly inspired me and a lot of people who knew her. As I sat listening to the preacher talk about her, I realized that she is not the only one with this kind of story. When I went home, me and my friend talked about it. I asked him why is it that great inventions like rocket ships, automated cars, and artificial intelligence exist, but there is no cure for cancer yet. We live in a world where we progress more trying to talk to aliens than totally outsmarting cells that turn on us. It’s sad, but actually, this tells us just how amazing technology actually is because it can do both even if one process is slower than the other. Through the means of technology; chemotherapy, immunotherapy, and AI-detectors that detect cancer cells before they multiply exists now thanks to persevering researchers, developers, and biologists. It’s so amazing that if people from 50 years ago look at the solutions humans have developed for cancer during modern times, to them it would look like a cure—it would look like hope. After all, flu was a deadly thing centuries ago. Now, I’m writing this blog with a headache and nonstop sniffing. This is me experiencing the product of technology in real time, and personally speaking, it’s an underrated privilege. And I've come to realize that if ever I choose a job, I want to help people experience that same privilege, especially in honor to my church mate who did not experience a direct cure to her sickness, and to a lot of people experiencing the same thing.



The Human In Technology

People have always been curious about how the world works, and math and science are just tools we use to explore that curiosity. Without Thomas Edison, we’d probably still be lighting candles, and without the Greeks, we wouldn’t even have trigonometry—the same math that helps keep buildings standing today. Even now, researchers are still working nonstop to understand and fight cancer. That’s when it hit me: math and science aren’t proof that humans are geniuses, they’re proof that humans care enough to keep asking questions. Every discovery adds another layer to the world we live in, slowly making life safer, easier, and more comfortable. Being curious and being helpful are almost the same thing. So technology isn’t some cold, robotic field meant only for prodigies or unbeatable chess players. At its core, it’s just people who are curious, persistent, and trying to make life a little better. And maybe someday I can do the same thing.
