Why Guitars Have Good Parenting Skills

Written By: Penny Wrinkle

Date: 02/03/2026





An Unexpected Pillar of Strength

Burnout is an inevitable part of being a student—unless one is somehow superhuman. People cope in different ways: some overeat, some under-eat, and some simply withdraw. I cope through an object: my white guitar. It may sound materialistic, but this instrument has taught me lessons about patience, persistence, and passion that feel almost human. Over time, it has become more than a tool; it has become a companion in navigating the challenges of life and study. Looking at my hands now, scarred and callused from hours of practice, I realize just how much they reflect the effort and lessons behind every chord I play.



An Object As A Lifelong Companion

Let me begin by saying, for some reason, my grandmother cherished my hands. When I was a little younger than I was, my grandmother would not let me wash the dishes, and she says it was to keep my hands youthful. She would always compare her hands to mine, pointing out how wrinkly hers was. That wish was shattered when my uncle chose to gift me a guitar now that I’m in college. Unfortunately, since I’ve owned a guitar, the tips of my fingers have become callused and layers of skin would willingly un-attach itself from my fingers. I would purposefully make my cousins uncomfortable by grazing the tip of my fingers onto their arms and they would get chills by how scaly it felt, that’s just how bad it is. With the way my hands look, nobody would dare hold them, yet ironically, I could play the most romantic songs if I wanted to. I have to thank my calluses because playing the guitar became less painful. Whenever I get tired of wrestling with complex concepts—especially programming—I just pick it up and play, and somehow, I feel better almost instantly. Both guitar and programming take practice, but guitar feels more rewarding because you can hear your progress right away. Press the strings correctly, and it sounds beautiful. Programming only really clicks after hours of squeezing out brain juice and figuring out some bigger life purpose.



Pain As The Counterpart of Passion

Even if guitar is seen as a creative hobby, it subtly teaches you about life—that success and satisfaction can only be felt after you’ve endured enough pain and learnt enough lessons to experience reward. Guitar is supposed to be my little escape, but it doesn’t let me fully relax. Even when I play, I still have to keep practicing, keep getting better. It’s like life doesn’t really give us a break. Even the “fun” things push you to improve, like the universe just wants you to keep leveling up. Guitar reminds me that passion matters. Even if it hurts, even if it’s frustrating, you keep going—because you want it to sound good, because you want to get better at something. And eventually, you can look at your hands—ugly, callused, maybe a little scaly—and think that these very hands worked for it, and that’s kind of satisfying. That’s what the little things, like guitar, teach you.
